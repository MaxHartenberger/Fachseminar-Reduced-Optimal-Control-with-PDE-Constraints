\begin{abstract}
We study a linear-quadratic optimal control problem governed by the Poisson equation with homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions. The control acts as a volumetric source restricted to a tagged subdomain $\omega\subset\Omega$, and a Tikhonov term with parameter $\beta$ regularizes the control in the $L^2(\Omega)$ metric. After P1 finite element discretization on a sequence of pre-generated meshes, we eliminate the state and work with the reduced functional $F(u)$, whose gradient is expressed through an adjoint solve and the control-space mass matrix.

On each mesh we compute the minimizer $u_*$ in two ways: (i) by solving the symmetric positive definite stationarity system with the Conjugate Gradient method (CG), and (ii) by applying first-order optimization methods to the reduced problem, namely Barzilai--Borwein (BB), fixed-step gradient descent with step size $1/L$, and a constant-parameter Nesterov scheme based on $(m,L)$. We report convergence histories in terms of the $U$-norm of the reduced gradient, as well as a mesh-independence study comparing objective values, norms of $u_*$ and $y(u_*)$, and iteration counts across mesh sizes.

The results show stable optimal values as the mesh is refined and highlight the efficiency advantage of CG for the quadratic reduced problem, while BB and accelerated gradient methods provide competitive first-order baselines.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem Statement}
We consider a linear-quadratic optimal control problem governed by the Poisson equation on a polygonal domain \(\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^2\) (in computations, typically \([0,1]^2\)). A circular subregion \(\omega\subset\Omega\) with nonzero Lebesgue measure is tagged on the mesh and serves as the support of the control. The control acts as a volumetric source term restricted to \(\omega\):
\begin{align}
  -\Delta y &= \chi_{\omega}\, u \quad\text{in } \Omega,\\
  y &= 0 \quad\text{on } \partial\Omega,
\end{align}
where \(\chi_{\omega}\) denotes the characteristic function of \(\omega\). We denote by \(V=H_0^1(\Omega)\) the state space and by \(U=L^2(\Omega)\) the control space. For a given target \(y_d\in L^2(\Omega)\) and a regularization parameter \(\beta>0\), we minimize
\begin{equation}
  J(y,u) = \tfrac12 \int_{\Omega} (y - y_d)^2\,dx + \tfrac{\beta}{2} \int_{\Omega} u^2\,dx.
\end{equation}
The target is chosen as the pyramid-like profile
\begin{equation}
  y_d(x) = 0.5 - \max\big(|x_1-0.5|,\,|x_2-0.5|\big),
\end{equation}
which belongs to \(L^2(\Omega)\). The control enters only on \(\omega\) via \(\chi_{\omega}\), so the effective source equals \(u\) on \(\omega\) and vanishes on \(\Omega\setminus\omega\). The PDE with homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions is well-posed for \(u\in L^2(\Omega)\), yielding a unique weak solution \(y\in V\) by Lax--Milgram.

\section{Variational Formulation and Discretization}
\paragraph{Weak formulation.} Define the bilinear and linear forms
\begin{align}
  a(y,v) &= \int_{\Omega} \nabla y\cdot\nabla v\, dx\quad\text{for }y,v\in V, & b(u,v) &= \int_{\omega} u\, v\, dx\quad\text{for }v\in U, v\in V.
\end{align}
For any \(u\in L^2(\Omega)\), the weak state equation reads: find \(y\in V\) such that
\begin{equation}
  a(y,v) = b(u,v) \quad \forall v\in V.
\end{equation}
By continuity and coercivity of \(a(\cdot,\cdot)\) on \(V\) and boundedness of \(b(\cdot,\cdot)\), this admits a unique \(y\in V\).

\paragraph{Finite elements.} 
Let \(\mathcal{T}_h\) be a conforming triangulation of \(\Omega\). We use the conforming P1 space \(V_h\subset V\) with nodal basis \(\{\varphi_i\}_{i=1}^{n}\). The control is represented in the same P1 basis but measured with the \(L^2\) inner product (``control metric''). We assemble the matrices
\begin{align}
  A_{ij} &= \int_{\Omega} \nabla \varphi_j\cdot\nabla \varphi_i\, dx, &
  M_{ij} &= \int_{\Omega} \varphi_j\, \varphi_i\, dx, &
  B_{ij} &= \int_{\omega} \varphi_j\, \varphi_i\, dx, &
  (M_U)_{ij} &= \int_{\Omega} \varphi_j\, \varphi_i\, dx.
\end{align}
Here, \(A\) is the stiffness matrix for state/adjoint solves, \(M\) is the state mass matrix used to weight the tracking term, $B$ maps control coefficients to the state RHS via restriction to \(\omega\), and $M_U$ is the control-space mass matrix defining the \(L^2\) inner product in \(U_h\).

\paragraph{Boundary conditions.} 
Homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions are imposed on the state/adjoint by modifying \(A\) and setting the RHS at boundary degrees of freedom to zero when solving. Importantly, no boundary conditions are applied to \(B\) or $M_U$; these operators remain the unmodified \(L^2\)-based forms on \(\Omega\).

\paragraph{Discrete state equation and objective.} 
With coefficient vectors \(\mathbf{y},\mathbf{u}\in\mathbb{R}^n\) and target coefficients \(\mathbf{y}_d\in\mathbb{R}^n\), the discrete state equation and cost read
\begin{align}
  A\mathbf{y} &= B\mathbf{u}, \\
  J(\mathbf{y},\mathbf{u}) &= \tfrac12 (\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{y}_d)^\top M (\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{y}_d) + \tfrac{\beta}{2} \mathbf{u}^\top M_U\mathbf{u}.
\end{align}

\paragraph{Adjoint and gradient.} In coefficients, the adjoint solves
\begin{equation}
  A\, \mathbf{p} = M\, (\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u}) - \mathbf{y}_d),
\end{equation}
and the gradient with respect to the control space inner product 
\begin{equation}
	\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle_U := \mathbf{a}^\top M_U\, \mathbf{b}, \qquad \|\mathbf{a}\|_U := \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle_U}.
\end{equation}
induced by \(M_U\) is
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}) = M_U^{-1}\nabla F(\mathbf{u}) = M_U^{-1}\, B^\top \mathbf{p} + \beta\, \mathbf{u}.
\end{equation}
The first-order optimality condition \(\nabla F(\mathbf{u}_*)=0\) is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
  B^\top \mathbf{p}_* + \beta\, M_U\, \mathbf{u}_* = 0, \qquad A\, \mathbf{p}_* = M\, (\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u}_*)-\mathbf{y}_d).
\end{equation}
and since $M_U$ is symmetric positive definite (SPD) it is also equivalent to $\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_*) = 0$

\paragraph{Hessian, convexity, and Lipschitz constant.} The reduced Hessian applied to \(\mathbf{v}\) is
\begin{equation}
  H\, \mathbf{v} = M_U^{-1}\, B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B\, \mathbf{v} + \beta\, \mathbf{v}.
\end{equation}
Since \(A\), \(M\), and \(M_U\) are symmetric positive definite on their natural subspaces and \(B\) is linear, the quadratic form associated with \(H\) is strictly positive for \(\beta>0\); thus, \(F\) is strictly convex with a unique minimizer. In the \(U\)-metric, the gradient is Lipschitz with constant
\begin{equation}
  L = \big\| M_U^{-1/2} B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B\, M_U^{-1/2} \big\|_2 + \beta,
\end{equation}
namely the spectral radius of the SPD term plus \(\beta\).


We first rewrite the reduced problem as a quadratic optimization problem in the control coefficients. Using the discrete operators, the objective
\[
  F(\mathbf{u}) = \tfrac12 \big(A^{-1} B\, \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{y}_d\big)^\top M \big(A^{-1} B\, \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{y}_d\big) + \tfrac{\beta}{2}\, \mathbf{u}^\top M_U\, \mathbf{u}
\]
expands to the unconstrained QP
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\mathbf{u}\in\mathbb{R}^n} \; \tfrac12\, \mathbf{u}^\top Q\, \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{c}^\top \mathbf{u} + \text{const},
  \qquad Q = B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B + \beta\, M_U,\quad \mathbf{c} = -\, B^\top A^{-1} M\, \mathbf{y}_d,
\end{equation}
with constant term $\text{const} = \tfrac12\, \mathbf{y}_d^\top M\, \mathbf{y}_d$. Since $A$, $M$, and $M_U$ are SPD on their natural subspaces, $Q$ is SPD, so the QP is strictly convex with unique minimizer characterized by the normal equations
\begin{equation}
  Q\, \mathbf{u}_* = B^\top A^{-1} M\, \mathbf{y}_d.
\end{equation}

The gradient used by all methods is the Riesz-represented gradient in \(U\):
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}) = M_U^{-1}\, B^\top \mathbf{p} + \beta\, \mathbf{u}, \quad \text{where } A\, \mathbf{p} = M\, (\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u}) - \mathbf{y}_d),\; A\, \mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u}) = B\, \mathbf{u}.
\end{equation}
Unless stated otherwise, we initialize at \(\mathbf{u}_0 = \nabla_U F(\mathbf{0})\) (as in the code) and stop when \(\|\nabla F(\mathbf{u}_k)\|_U \le \texttt{tol}\) or a maximum number of iterations is reached.

\paragraph{Lipschitz constant $L$ and strong convexity modulus $m$.}
In the $U$-metric, $H$ is self-adjoint and SPD, and can be written as $H = M_U^{-1} Q$ with
\(Q = B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B + \beta\, M_U\). Therefore, the extreme eigenvalues of $H$ in the $U$-inner product are the extreme \emph{generalized} eigenvalues of the pencil $(Q,M_U)$:
\begin{equation}
	Q\,\mathbf{v} = \lambda\, M_U\,\mathbf{v}.
\end{equation}
The Lipschitz constant is $L=\lambda_{\max}$ and the strong convexity constant is $m=\lambda_{\min}$. Numerically, we approximate $L$ either by power iteration applied to $H$ using the $U$-Rayleigh quotient, or by solving the generalized eigenproblem for the largest eigenpair; for $(m,L)$ we solve the generalized eigenproblem for both extremes.

For the strongly convex quadratic QP with Hessian $Q$ in the $U$-metric, let
$L=\lambda_{\max}$ and $m=\lambda_{\min}$ denote the largest/smallest generalized eigenvalues of the pencil $(Q, M_U)$,
equivalently the spectrum of $H = M_U^{-1/2} Q M_U^{-1/2}$.

\section{Reduced Formulation}

\paragraph{State elimination and reduced functional.}
The discrete PDE constraint $A\mathbf{y}=B\mathbf{u}$ defines the linear control-to-state map
\(\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u})=A^{-1}B\mathbf{u}\). Substituting into the discrete objective yields the reduced functional
\begin{equation}
  F(\mathbf{u})
  = \tfrac12 \big(\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u})-\mathbf{y}_d\big)^\top M\big(\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u})-\mathbf{y}_d\big)
    + \tfrac{\beta}{2} \mathbf{u}^\top M_U\mathbf{u}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Derivative vs. $U$-gradient (Riesz representative).}
We equip the discrete control space $U_h\cong\mathbb{R}^n$ with the inner product
\begin{equation}
  \langle \mathbf{a},\mathbf{b} \rangle_U := \mathbf{a}^\top M_U\mathbf{b},
  \qquad \|\mathbf{a}\|_U := \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{a},\mathbf{a} \rangle_U}.
\end{equation}
The (Fr\'echet) derivative $F'(\mathbf{u})$ is a linear functional on $U_h$, i.e., $F'(\mathbf{u})\in U_h^*$. The $U$-gradient
\(\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u})\in U_h\) is defined as the Riesz representative of $F'(\mathbf{u})$ with respect to $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_U$:
\begin{equation}
  \langle \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}),\mathbf{v} \rangle_U = F'(\mathbf{u})[\mathbf{v}] \qquad \forall \mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^n.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Connection to $\nabla F$ in coefficient coordinates.}
Sometimes it is convenient to identify the derivative $F'(\mathbf{u})$ with a vector via the standard Euclidean inner product $\langle \mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}\rangle_2=\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{b}$. We denote this coordinate gradient by $\nabla F(\mathbf{u})\in\mathbb{R}^n$, defined through
\begin{equation}
  F'(\mathbf{u})[\mathbf{v}] = \langle \nabla F(\mathbf{u}),\mathbf{v} \rangle_2 = \nabla F(\mathbf{u})^\top \mathbf{v} \qquad \forall \mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^n.
\end{equation}
Comparing with the defining identity for $\nabla_U F$ gives the Riesz-map relation
\begin{equation}
  \nabla F(\mathbf{u}) = M_U\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Adjoint formula and explicit expression.}
Using the adjoint variable $\mathbf{p}$ defined by
\begin{equation}
  A\mathbf{p} = M\big(\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u})-\mathbf{y}_d\big),
\end{equation}
one obtains for all directions $\mathbf{v}$
\begin{equation}
  F'(\mathbf{u})[\mathbf{v}] = \big(B^\top\mathbf{p}+\beta M_U\mathbf{u}\big)^\top\mathbf{v}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, the $U$-gradient (i.e., the Riesz representative in the $M_U$-metric) is
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}) = M_U^{-1}B^\top\mathbf{p} + \beta\mathbf{u}.
\end{equation}
The optimality condition is
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_*)=\mathbf{0}
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  B^\top\mathbf{p}_* + \beta M_U\mathbf{u}_* = \mathbf{0}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Quadratic structure and stationarity system.}
Since $\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{u})$ depends linearly on $\mathbf{u}$, the reduced problem is a strictly convex quadratic program.
Indeed, with the control-to-state operator $S:=A^{-1}B$ we have
\begin{align}
  F(\mathbf{u})
  &= \tfrac12 (S\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{y}_d)^\top M(S\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{y}_d) + \tfrac{\beta}{2}\mathbf{u}^\top M_U\mathbf{u}\\
  &= \tfrac12\mathbf{u}^\top \big(S^\top M S + \beta M_U\big)\mathbf{u} - \big(S^\top M\mathbf{y}_d\big)^\top \mathbf{u} + \tfrac12\mathbf{y}_d^\top M\mathbf{y}_d.
\end{align}
Using $S=A^{-1}B$ and symmetry of $A$, this can be written as the unconstrained quadratic program
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\mathbf{u}\in\mathbb{R}^n}\; \tfrac12\mathbf{u}^\top Q\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{b}^\top \mathbf{u} + \text{const},
  \qquad Q := B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B + \beta M_U,\quad \mathbf{b} := B^\top A^{-1} M\mathbf{y}_d.
\end{equation}
Since $B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B$ is symmetric positive semidefinite and $\beta M_U$ is symmetric positive definite for $\beta>0$, it follows that $Q$ is symmetric positive definite, hence $F$ is strictly convex.
Here the constant term is $\text{const}=\tfrac12\mathbf{y}_d^\top M\mathbf{y}_d$, which does not depend on $\mathbf{u}$ and therefore does not influence the minimizer.
Eliminating $\mathbf{y}$ and $\mathbf{p}$ from the optimality condition yields the symmetric positive definite (SPD) linear system
\begin{equation}
  Q\mathbf{u}_* = \mathbf{b}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Lipschitz constant $L$ and strong convexity modulus $m$.}
The constants $L$ and $m$ quantify smoothness and strong convexity of the reduced functional in the control metric. More precisely, $L$ is the Lipschitz constant of the $U$-gradient,
\begin{equation}
  \|\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u})-\nabla_U F(\mathbf{v})\|_U \le L\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|_U \qquad \forall \mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^n,
\end{equation}
and $m>0$ is the strong convexity modulus,
\begin{equation}
  F(\mathbf{v}) \ge F(\mathbf{u}) + \langle \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}),\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}\rangle_U + \tfrac{m}{2}\|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}\|_U^2 \qquad \forall \mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^n.
\end{equation}
For the quadratic reduced problem, the $U$-gradient's $U$-Hessian is
\begin{equation}
  H\mathbf{v} = \nabla_U^2 F(\mathbf{u})\mathbf{v} = M_U^{-1}Q\mathbf{v} = M_U^{-1} B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B\mathbf{v} + \beta\mathbf{v}.
\end{equation}
This operator is self-adjoint and SPD with respect to $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_U$. Accordingly, $m$ and $L$ are the extreme generalized eigenvalues of the pencil $(Q,M_U)$,
\begin{equation}
  Q\mathbf{v} = \lambda M_U\mathbf{v}, \qquad m=\lambda_{\min},\; L=\lambda_{\max},
\end{equation}
equivalently the extreme eigenvalues of the SPD matrix $M_U^{-1/2}QM_U^{-1/2}$. Their ratio $\kappa=L/m$ is the condition number that governs convergence rates of GD and Nesterov in the next section.

\section{Optimization Methods}
Unless stated otherwise, we initialize at $\mathbf{u}_0=\nabla_U F(\mathbf{0})$ (as in the code) and stop when $\|\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_k)\|_U\le\texttt{tol}_{abs}+\texttt{tol}_{rel}\|\nabla_U F(\mathbf{0})\|_U$ with $\texttt{tol}_{abs}=10^{-5}$ and $\texttt{tol}_{rel}=10^{-3}$ or a maximum number of 1000 iterations is reached.
\subsection{Steepest Descent/ Gradient method with constant Stepsize \(\alpha = 1/L\) (GD)}
For an \(L\)-Lipschitz gradient in the \(U\)-metric, the fixed-step update
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{u}_{k+1} = \mathbf{u}_k - \alpha\, \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_k), \quad \alpha = 1/L.
\end{equation}


\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Steepest Descent with constant stepsize $\alpha=1/L$}\label{alg:gd}
\KwData{tolerance $\texttt{tol}>0$, $\texttt{max\_iter}$, model with $\nabla_U F$, $\|\cdot\|_U$}
\KwResult{sequence $(\mathbf{u}_k)$ converging to a minimizer}
$\mathbf{u} \gets \mathbf{u}_0=\nabla_U F(\mathbf{0})$\\
Estimate $L$ by power iteration; $\alpha \gets 1/L$\;
\For{$k=0,1,\dots$}{
  $\mathbf{g} \gets \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u})$; $g_n \gets \|\mathbf{g}\|_U$\;
  \If{$g_n \le \texttt{tol}$}{break}
  $\mathbf{u} \gets \mathbf{u} - \alpha\, \mathbf{g}$\;
}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Steepest Descent with Barzilai-Borwein stepsize strategy (BB)}
BB methods approximate the inverse curvature along the last step by selecting the steplength from secant information in the \(U\)-inner product. Define \(\mathbf{s}_k=\mathbf{u}_k-\mathbf{u}_{k-1}\), \(\mathbf{d}_k=\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_k)-\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_{k-1})\), and the \(U\)-inner product \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_U\). The two classical choices are
\begin{align}
  \alpha_k^{\text{BB1}} &= \frac{\langle \mathbf{d}_k, \mathbf{d}_k \rangle_U}{\langle \mathbf{s}_k, \mathbf{d}_k \rangle_U}, &
  \alpha_k^{\text{BB2}} &= \frac{\langle \mathbf{s}_k, \mathbf{s}_k \rangle_U}{\langle \mathbf{s}_k, \mathbf{d}_k \rangle_U}.
\end{align}
We alternate BB1/BB2 per iteration and fall back to \(\alpha_k=1\) if \(\big|\langle \mathbf{s}_k, \mathbf{d}_k \rangle_U\big|\) is numerically tiny. The update is
\(\mathbf{u}_{k+1} = \mathbf{u}_k - (1/\alpha_k)\, \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_k)\).

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Steepest Descent with Barzilai-Borwein stepsize strategy}\label{alg:bb}
\KwData{$\texttt{tol}>0$, $\texttt{max\_iter}$, model with $\nabla_U F$, $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_U$, $\|\cdot\|_U$}
\KwResult{sequence $(\mathbf{u}_k)$}
$\mathbf{u}_{-1} \gets \mathbf{0}$; $\mathbf{g}_{-1} \gets \nabla_U F(\mathbf{0})$\;
$\mathbf{u}_0 \gets \mathbf{u}_0=\nabla_U F(\mathbf{0})$\; $\mathbf{g}_0 \gets \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_0)$\\
\For{$k=0,1,\dots$}{
  \If{$\|\mathbf{g}_k\|_U \le \texttt{tol}$}{break}
  $\mathbf{s}_k \gets \mathbf{u}_k - \mathbf{u}_{k-1}$; $\mathbf{d}_k \gets \mathbf{g}_k - \mathbf{g}_{k-1}$; $s\!d \gets \langle \mathbf{s}_k, \mathbf{d}_k \rangle_U$\;
  \eIf{$|s\!d| < \varepsilon$}{
    $\alpha_k \gets 1$\;
  }{
    \eIf{$k$ is even}{
      $\alpha_k \gets \langle \mathbf{d}_k, \mathbf{d}_k \rangle_U / s\!d$\;
    }{
      $\alpha_k \gets s\!d / \max\{\langle \mathbf{s}_k, \mathbf{s}_k \rangle_U, \varepsilon\}$\;
    }
  }
  $\mathbf{u}_{k+1} \gets \mathbf{u}_k - (1/\alpha_k)\, \mathbf{g}_k$\;
  $\mathbf{u}_{k-1} \gets \mathbf{u}_k$; $\mathbf{g}_{k-1} \gets \mathbf{g}_k$\;
  $\mathbf{u}_k \gets \mathbf{u}_{k+1}$; $\mathbf{g}_k \gets \nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_k)$\;
}
\end{algorithm}


\subsection{Nesterov Acceleration with constant parameters}
Set
\[\alpha = 1/L,\qquad \kappa=L/m,\qquad \beta = \frac{\sqrt{\kappa}-1}{\sqrt{\kappa}+1}.\]
Then the constant-parameter Nesterov scheme reads
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{y}_k &= \mathbf{u}_k + \beta(\mathbf{u}_k - \mathbf{u}_{k-1}), &
  \mathbf{u}_{k+1} &= \mathbf{y}_k - \alpha \nabla_U F(\mathbf{y}_k).
\end{align}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Nesterov Acceleration with constant parameters}\label{alg:nest-ml}
\KwData{$\texttt{tol}>0$, $\texttt{max\_iter}$, model with $\nabla_U F$, $M_U$, Hessian action $H$}
\KwResult{sequence $(\mathbf{u}_k)$}
Estimate $(L,m)$ from generalized eigenpairs of $(Q, M_U)$; $\alpha \gets 1/L$; $\kappa \gets L/m$; $\beta \gets (\sqrt{\kappa}-1)/(\sqrt{\kappa}+1)$

$\mathbf{u}_{-1} \gets \mathbf{u}_0$; $\mathbf{u} \gets \mathbf{u}_0$\;
\For{$k=0,1,\dots$}{
  $\mathbf{y} \gets \mathbf{u} + \beta (\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}_{k-1})$\; $\mathbf{g} \gets \nabla_U F(\mathbf{y})$; $g_n \gets \|\mathbf{g}\|_U$\;
  \If{$g_n \le \texttt{tol}$}{break}
  $\mathbf{u}_{k+1} \gets \mathbf{y} - \alpha\, \mathbf{g}$\;
}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Conjugate Gradient method (CG) for the Stationarity System}
The reduced problem is strictly convex and quadratic. The first-order optimality condition $\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_*)=0$ can be written as an SPD linear system in the control unknowns,
\begin{equation}
  Q\mathbf{u}_* = B^\top A^{-1} M\mathbf{y}_d, \qquad Q := B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B + \beta M_U.
\end{equation}
In the implementation, we solve this system with the Conjugate Gradient method in a matrix-free manner (Hessian action only). Concretely, we apply $Q\mathbf{v}$ via the reduced Hessian action and the control metric, and run the standard CG recurrences while tracking the residual.
We use a standard relative residual stopping criterion (as in SciPy's \texttt{scipy.sparse.linalg.cg}): with residual $\mathbf{r}_k:=\mathbf{b}-Q\mathbf{u}_k$, we stop once
\[
  \|\mathbf{r}_k\|_2 \le \texttt{rtol}\,\|\mathbf{b}\|_2,
\]
and we set $\texttt{rtol}=10^{-4}$ in all runs.
Moreover, since $\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u})=M_U^{-1}(Q\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{b})=-M_U^{-1}\mathbf{r}$, the reduced gradient norm can be obtained directly from the residual via
\[
  \|\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_k)\|_U = \sqrt{\mathbf{r}_k^\top M_U^{-1}\mathbf{r}_k},
\]
which we record as the convergence curve in Figure~\ref{fig:convergence}.

\begin{algorithm}[]
\caption{CG for $Q\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{b}$ (matrix-free)}\label{alg:cg}
\KwData{model with $A^{-1}$ solves, matrices $B,M,M_U$, Hessian action $H$; $\texttt{rtol}>0$}
\KwResult{$\mathbf{u}_*$}
$\mathbf{b} \gets B^\top A^{-1}(M\mathbf{y}_d)$\;
Define operator $Q\mathbf{v} := M_U(H\mathbf{v})$ (equivalently $B^\top A^{-1} M A^{-1} B\mathbf{v} + \beta M_U\mathbf{v}$)\;
Call CG to solve $Q\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{b}$ to relative tolerance $\texttt{rtol}=10^{-4}$\;
\end{algorithm}

\section{Numerical Results}
We report results on a representative external mesh with size $h=\meshhtoken$ (here $n=3093$ control degrees of freedom). We use control region tag $\omega=2$ and Tikhonov parameter $\beta=10^{-3}$. The first-order methods (BB, GD, Nesterov) are initialized at $\mathbf{u}_0=\nabla_U F(\mathbf{0})$ and stopped when $\|\nabla_U F(\mathbf{u}_k)\|_U \le \texttt{tol}_{abs}+\texttt{tol}_{rel}\|\nabla_U F(\mathbf{0})\|_U$ with $\texttt{tol}_{abs}=10^{-5}$ and $\texttt{tol}_{rel}=10^{-3}$. In addition, we solve the SPD stationarity system $Q\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{b}$ with CG.

\paragraph{Calculation and results for $m$ and $L$.}
The smoothness/strong-convexity constants are defined as the extreme generalized eigenvalues of $(Q,M_U)$,
\(Q\mathbf{v}=\lambda M_U\mathbf{v}\), i.e. $L=\lambda_{\max}$ and $m=\lambda_{\min}$. In the implementation, both are estimated with ARPACK (\texttt{eigsh}) applied to the generalized eigenproblem using a matrix-free $Q$-operator.
On the representative mesh $h=0.02$ we obtain
\[
  L \approx 1.3163607903\times 10^{-3},\qquad m \approx 1.0000000015\times 10^{-3},\qquad \kappa = L/m \approx 1.31636.
\]
As expected for $Q=B^\top A^{-1}MA^{-1}B + \beta M_U$ with $\beta>0$, the smallest generalized eigenvalue is close to $\beta$.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
On this quadratic reduced problem, CG achieves a high-accuracy solution of $Q\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{b}$ in a modest number of Krylov iterations, and its final cost matches the first-order methods. BB, GD($1/L$), and Nesterov reach the stopping threshold in only a few iterations on this mesh, but the overall runtime is influenced by the one-time spectral estimates needed for fixed-step GD and $(m,L)$-Nesterov (Table~\ref{tab:timings_h002}). The optimized control and state (Figure~\ref{fig:final_fields}) provide a qualitative view of the solution on the representative mesh.
